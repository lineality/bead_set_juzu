Principles & Recommendations

The Principle of Feedback
Signal-Exchange is a subtle and nuanced part of breathing. The Book 'A World Appears' by Michael Pollan (a book about the science of consciousness, not claiming to be a book about buddhist or hindu scriptures) is an excellent narrative that touches on a number of topics around how illusive the topic of consciousness, and simply being aware (let alone being aware of consciousness), can be, and in a wonderfully accessible narrative. His book on "how to change your mind" also touches on aspects of what STEM is learning about the Default Mode Network (DMN).


The Principle of Exchange

There is something different about a process that involves exchange, such as sharing or teaching (what might abstractly be called a 'signal'). A saying from the (also strangely associated with another school of Japanese Zen) Komuso-Shakuhachi music-monks is that you only 'have' a song when you are teaching, or sharing, it with someone else. 

Sharing feedback (which is itself a feedback signal) with a family memory or friend of acquaintance or stranger, or possibly a tree or a lake, is in some ways different from the inner-solitary aspects that are at least in the west assumed to be more a part of mindful zazen. 

On another level, as Dogen describes, some parts of zazen are parts of processes of patterns and signals in the natural world outside and inside of us.


The Principle of ~Equivalent-Process (maybe not the best term)

The analogy of the moonlit lake:
Imagine you take a walk about a moonlit lake at night in the autumn. The first step you take in the process of walking around the lake is no less 'moonlit lake' than the last step. When you are done, and the moon is gone, you do not have any more 'moonlit lake' than when you were walking. Moonlit lake is somehow equally (but not absolutely uniformly in every way, each part is unique) embedded through a process, a practice. 

Bringing this analogy back to breathing, and being aware of your breathing, in some ways there is an equal value and contribution in your first zazen breaths as there are through and after a lifetime of dedicated practice. Some aspects are different, but some aspects are not. The moonlit lake is somehow equivalent across a process made of very different and unique moments and parts. 




Interpretation, Nuance & Presumption
A running theme in mindful-breathing-zazen practice is trying not to make too many presumptions, and learning to be aware of the presumptions that you did not know you had. 

This does not mean you should never make decisions or recognitions or judgement calls: if you realized you are meditating in a burning building, or on the coast just before a tsunami, you should immediately flee to safety. 



In each culture and time period there are probably concepts that pre-occupy various people. Maybe it is shame or pride or superiority or inferiority or guilt or resentment or some other agenda that appears so 'fish in water' normal that we do not even see that it is a preoccupation particular with the geitzgeist (~spirit of the times)  in which we are swimming. 

In the USA such a background assumption is probably a return-on-investment ROI calculation: this zazen better be worthy my time, I better be getting something out of this zazen, I need to finish and achieve this zazen, this zazen needs to be a good accompaniment with a nice gold trophy and a certificate, I need to be getting better at this, I need to be winning something, I need to be getting that achievement, I need to move towards winning the zazen, I need to dominate all those other beginner noobs and have more zazen and better zazen than they do, etc. Or conversely: I can't do this. I'm just starting out. I don't feel anything happening. This isn't working. This will take so long to accomplish and I don't even know what that finished in-hand accomplishment of zazen looks like. This won't ever work, I'm just starting out. What should all this bio-feedback be finally producing? 

Another presumption may be that 'just breathing' or 'just being aware' does not involve fingers, and somehow requires isolation from the world. The two presumptions may both be calamitously backwards. 

As in the analogy of The moon and the lake for Dogen's concept of the start of a process being in some ways comparable to any other part of a process, many parts of zazen, or mindfulness, or signal-feedback, is a process across which the starting steps are (if confusingly) as significant and any later steps.


There is a lot of valuable lore and research in old texts, but figuring out how you should interpret and utilize the often time-clouded information can take an unexpectedly convoluted and lengthy process. 


For example, three somewhat sequential works of ~literature/history:

1. Saigyo's (to 1178?) writings at the beginning of the Genai period, expressing the value of nature and life and a need to integrate nature with society in wisdom.

2. The Heike Monotagari (1170-1200) about the Genpai period: one of the greatest expositions in all of literature about the value and substance of empty: a tale that is not only critical of the sport-maniac thrill-seeking that destroyed Japan, but also is highly critical (in my reading at least) of Buddhist practices that are overly disconnected from society, culture, nature, and positive value.

3. Just after the Genpei period: (Born in ~1200) Dogen's writings about practices that aim to help to prevent the disasters of the Genpei period. When Dogen writes about deluded judgement and practices to improve consciousness and the world, this is not hallmark card self-help rhetoric floating in space: there is a very very real context for what he is saying.

The later few centuries seem to have built on this somewhat uniquely japanese and uniquely pragmatic idea that practice to integrate with the world for positive outcomes (against pathology) producing ~literature that reinforces a society integrated into and connected to the world for positive culture and STEM: 
- Zeami (~1300), (creator of Noh, with his father, kanami)
- Sen-No Rikyu (to 1590),  (creator of the Japanese Tea Ceremony)
- Ryokan (to 1830), (a lovable and ridiculous zen/shinto odd-ball)

Another example of the valuable but convoluted and not-simple literature of Zen are 'Koan' collection, which in reality are not as picturesque as American pop-culture suggests ('The sound of one hand clapping').

Literacy and Learning:
It appears, from the history of vedism then hinduism, buddhism, and various "cross-pollinating" traditions such as Jainism, and the later evolution of Buddhism outside of 'India' (e.g. Tibet, China, Korea, Japan, Thailand, Vietnam, etc.)

An analogy or framework for this is phonetic literacy and math numeracy. Roughly 10,000 years before contemporary literacy and numeracy, sophisticated symbol systems were used for bookkeeping and trade and lending, but it took thousands of years until rare innovations were made and maintained to create the 'non-automatic' skills of literacy and numeracy that (generally) enhance what people can do. 

In the same way (as symbols and language did not automatically generate literacy), everyone breaths, but even basic specific breathing techniques were developed over a long time period. For example, (?) trying to stop breathing and trying to chant words repetitively seemed to be done long before people focused specifically on how they were breathing and how that related to mind-health. Like literacy and numeracy, progress builds on these new 'learned' skills (for example visualization plus breathing in Tibet).

A "juzu" bead-construct system is similar to but not entirely the same as either a charm/talisman/power-bracelet/necklace, or a rosary/meditation/thought/prayer/counter, in the same way that a tarot-deck-system (of which there are many, each with many ways of being used) is similar to but not exactly the same as an art-card-set, a card-game-set, or a prediction/divination system.

- there appears to be a "system" (as in tarot being 'a system')
- there are some but limited data on what Juzu are used for
- there are some but limited 'how to' data
- most "how to" details are unclear

1. Model in Brief
(an animated-robot for time-travel in nonlinear time)
A juzu (specific type (family of types) of bead construct) system is:
- a non-anthropomorphic living robot-doll of the 'unseen' variety and of the bonded variety. 
- practiced broadly/diversely in 'common standard range' of "practices"(broad sense) trance, obe, invocation/summoning, banishment, ~divination, active-imagination, ancestor work, etc. 
- operates chiefly via facilitated (world)portals and "time" manipulation

Part 2: First pass, ice breaker

Note:

Note on holding the bead-set and advancing beads:
Holdset in left (or right) hand, with on bead between your thumb and index or middle finger (different sects disagree). Sometime you will move from bead to bead. Sometimes you will rotate a single bead. Sometime you will simply hold the bead-set. You can let the rest of the beadset hang, hold it in your other hand, or loop it around the same hand. Whatever is best for you in the context.

Using 108 bead set:

Pause and Give Away
Starting on the main-hub bead, and again whenever you get to a spacer-bead or a hub, pause from whatever you had been doing and express "giving-away": give away the bead set holding it out and bowing your head, for the duration of one or two breaths.

For each of 108-beads: breath in and out ~once:
with your awareness in-between and alternating between, watching and not watching your breathing, at whatever alternation.

Feedback
As you move from bead to bead with your fingers (your choice as to how) you may want to wrap the long hanging part of the beadset over a finger or two of the same hand you are using to hold the set - leaving your other hand free. 

As you breath in and out move your right hand (or not-holding-beads-hand) up and down following your breathing like a monitor - biofeedback. 

Optionally, the first time around you can do more yoga stretching and feedback as you breathe through the 108 beads.

Why beads?
One new-agey meme that floats around is the statement that 'all you need is already inside you.' Taken vaguely as a reminder to look at what resources you already have, or just a cheery smiley slogan, this is fair enough. But as a literal fundamentalism this is unhelpful and often crosses the line into a pejorative world-rejection - and specifically in a context of both secular and buddhist mindfulness, this runs contrary to a decentered-changing model of self which is part of self-observation, self-recognition, self-acceptance, and biofeedback. 

The mind is a maze that we have not fully mapped, but we do know that how tasks are combined has a major impact on what the mind does and how it does it. 
For example: breathing and counting your breaths 108 times vs. breathing and ticking through a 108 beads on a chord:
Here are two overlapping contexts:  
A. Multi-tasking by keeping track of a series of numbers (even a short set) while you do another task severely impairs what other tasks the brain is doing - and importantly in a way that we do not recognize at the time. (ref needed)
B. "System-1 and System-2" physical brain processes: Aside from theoretical  and ideological models of conscious and subconscious, repeatedly/routinely doing things moves different processes in and out of automatic vs. conscious brain areas. (Ref. Kahneman & Tversky et al) Adding and subtracting different tasks and focusing on different tasks makes an enormous difference in what happens in the brain and mind. 

While not as universal as 'singing,' the use of breathing/meditation/prayer beads/knots/etc has been maintained in numerous cultures over thousands of years (but so has alcoholism, so that alone means nothing). There has been an evolution of beadsets used for mindfulness-practice, and you can experiment for yourself to see what has what effects for you. Is a 108 set different from a 22 or 27 set? Do spacers (small beads amid the 108) make a difference? Do counters or tassels make a difference? Does shape or material or size make a difference? Does the sound they make make a difference?

Over time there have been hard-won and maintained discoveries of what works - as well as useless traditions maintained for no good reason. Experiment and see what works best for you. 

On the other hand, if you have an alternative method that actually works better than beads (not because of some ideology but based on long term empirical results) then by all means do what works best.

7. Proxies and Processes
Another reason to at least try to have a technique that is somewhat neutral is the prooblem that has emerged throughout history in such places as archetecture and music (note: even in the dictionary, 'modern' doesn't only or simply mean contemporary) namely that the people who design other people's experiences become so warped in their perceptions that they overtly and proudly believe that their job and role is to deliberately cause other people distress while following theoretical abstract principles with no basis in worldly reality. As in music people often come to focus on such items as exact timing and pitch and ever higher volume and speeds, or exact visual colors and shapes of their instrument, exact abstract categories of materials, etc., you might become fixated on the exact timing or depth or regularity of your breathing. For many people it seems counterintuitive to have a 'process' instead of a 'proxy' for a specific goal. Let's examine process vs. proxy briefly.

Non-Absolute and Non-Singular Frames of Reference
(Intro: What is a headset? And how can a beadset be a kind of un-clock in it's functions.)
As you rotate a bead, and breath or sutra, 
Let's start with the sciency idea that there is no absolute frame of reference - (which is oddly often combined with the idea that the sun is "the real" frame of reference and not the earth).
Start with the frame of reference just being you, with your eyes closed...it doesn't seem like much is moving (unless there's an earthquake or something) 
then move to the (perspective of the) earth being your frame of reference. You (the earth) don't seem to be moving, but everything around you is moving: the moon, the sun, the stars and planets (are moving) - sometimes the planets change direction whereas the stars mostly stay in their paths. The sun's path wonders from side to side as it goes around giving seasons by the earth's poles (your poles...unless you're on the equator). Zoom out to see how many of those stars have planets (often with moons), and there's a milky way galaxy (which you're in, that's where most visible stars are), which spins around you, and far away beyond those closer stars there are many more galaxies, etc. etc. and everything is spinning around you. 
- now move your frame of reference to 'the sun'. You are now one of many stars, each with solar systems, in some galaxy (usually). About 9 planets, an asteroid belt and an oort cloud (of comets etc) spin around you, all mostly in the same direction. The other sun-stars, the milky-way, and other galaxies move around you slowly, barely moving at all compared to your planets. 
- now imagine you are the (center of the) milky way galaxy, floating like a many armed octopus, with other galaxies around you, of various shapes, and out by the tip of one of your tentacles is 'the sun and the earth' and they circle around you.
- now shift your frame of reference to the bead you are rotating. like the planet, and the solar system, and the galaxy, the bead is rotating. Is the bead still, with 'the world' rotating around it? (Try and see it that way.) Or is 'the world' still and the bead is rotating? (Try and see from that perspective.) Or are they both rotating? How about the perspective of the inside of the bead? How about the chord? Is the chord rotating and polishing the inside of the bead, or is the bead rotating around the chord?
Can you imagine more than one of these frames of reference at the same time? Can you put yourself in some meta-location and see that each of these frames of reference can co-exist with the others?

In 曹洞宗 (Sōtō Zen) 数珠 (rosary), the metal ring (often called 月輪, kasshō or 輪, wa) serves a few key purposes:

Purpose of the Metal Ring


Symbolic Representation

The ring represents the moon, symbolizing clarity, enlightenment, and the cycles of life and practice.
It also signifies the unity of all things and the interconnectedness of the practitioner with the Dharma (Buddhist teachings).


Practical Function

It acts as a tactile marker to help count repetitions of mantras (e.g., Namu Shakyamuni Butsu) or prayers during meditation.
The ring separates groups of beads, making it easier to track counts without losing place.


How the Metal Ring Is Used


Counting Mantras or Prayers

Practitioners hold the rosary and slide one bead at a time toward the metal ring while reciting a mantra (e.g., "Namu Shakyamuni Butsu").
The ring itself is not counted—it marks the end of a cycle (e.g., after 108 beads, one full cycle of the rosary).


Meditation and Focus

The ring’s smooth, cool surface can be a focal point for grounding during meditation.
Some traditions use the ring to signify the moment of awakening—when the practitioner "rings the bell" of the mind, recognizing their true nature.


Rituals and Ceremonies

In Sōtō Zen, the metal ring may be used during dharma ceremonies or memorial services to symbolize the transmission of the Dharma.
Monks and lay practitioners may hold the ring between their fingers as a reminder of their vows.


Cultural Significance

The ring’s design varies, but it often features smooth, rounded edges to avoid disrupting meditation.
In some traditions, the ring is engraved with sutras or Zen symbols (e.g., the enso circle) to deepen its spiritual meaning.

The 月輪 (kasshō, "moon ring") in the 数珠 (rosary) is deeply tied to Dōgen Zenji (道元禅師), the founder of the Sōtō Zen school, but its exact origins in the rosary are part of a broader evolution. Here’s how Dōgen’s influence connects to the concept:

Dōgen’s Influence on the 月輪


Symbolism of the Moon in Dōgen’s Teachings

Dōgen frequently used the moon as a metaphor for enlightenment, clarity, and the boundless nature of reality in his writings, particularly in the Shōbōgenzō (e.g., "Genjōkōan" and "Gabyō").
He described the moon as a symbol of the true self and the Dharma body—something that is both ever-present and yet beyond grasp. This aligns with the ring’s role in the rosary as a marker of cycles and awakening.


Practical Use in Zen Practice

While Dōgen did not explicitly describe the kasshō in the context of the rosary in his surviving texts, his emphasis on mindfulness and the interplay of form and emptiness likely influenced how Zen practitioners integrated symbolic objects like the moon ring into their tools.
The kasshō became a physical manifestation of the moon’s symbolism—a tangible reminder of Dōgen’s teachings.


Evolution of the Rosary in Sōtō Zen

The number of beads (108) and the structure of the rosary (including the kasshō) were standardized later, but the concept of the moon ring as a spiritual marker reflects Dōgen’s broader philosophy.
The kasshō may have been adopted or formalized in the centuries after Dōgen’s time (13th century) as Zen practice and ritual tools became more codified.
