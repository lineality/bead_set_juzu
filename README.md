# Principles & Recommendations

First, a few concepts that should help with navigating the landscape of meditation and mindfulness, then a simple 'try this' recommended framework to get started with practice. 

### The Principle of an Ecosystem of Signals & Feedback:
Signal-Exchange is a subtle and nuanced part of breathing. The book 'A World Appears' by Michael Pollan (a book about the science of consciousness, not claiming to be a book about buddhist or hindu scriptures) is an excellent narrative that touches on a number of topics around how illusive the topics of conscious, and of simply being aware (let alone being aware of consciousness), can be, and in a wonderfully accessible narrative. His book on "how to change your mind" also touches on aspects of what STEM is learning about the mind (and meditation) including the Default Mode Network (DMN).

One way of looking at signals and feedback is having status information on your 'dashboard' when you are driving your car (or a market status dashboard if you are looking at your Bloomberg terminal). Driving a car, or a school bus, with no information about anything outside of the bus would be infeasible. Seeing out the windshield is a start. Having more windows is even better. Having mirrors, that can tell you even more about your 'space' and 'status.' Having dials on a dashboard that can tell you speed, temperature, RPMs, how much gas you have left!, that's very helpful. And being able to send and receive 'signals' from other drivers, like break-lights, backup-lights, and turn signals, that's even better. Without such status information our guestimations would end up being dangerously wrong. By letting the mind and body equilibrate and monitor itself, you are feeding status information into your system. With that status information, perhaps without you being consciously aware, your whole system is less 'driving in the dark.'


### The Principle of Exchange

There is something different about a process that involves exchange, such as sharing or teaching (what might abstractly be called a 'signal'). A saying from the Fuke-shu sect in Japan (Zen, Komuso-Shakuhachi flute music-monks) is that you only 'have' a song while / when you are teaching ('transmitting') it to someone else. 

Sharing feedback (which is itself a feedback signal) with a family member or friend or acquaintance or stranger, or possibly a tree or a lake, is in some ways different from the inner-solitary aspects that are (at least in the west) assumed to be more a part of mindful zazen (simple breathing). 

On another level, as Dogen describes, some parts of zazen are parts of processes of patterns and signals in the natural world outside of, and inside, of us.


The Principle of ~Equivalent-Process (maybe not the best term)

The analogy of the moonlit lake:
Imagine you take a walk around a moonlit lake at night in October. The first step you take in the process of walking around the lake is no less 'moonlit lake' than the last step. When you are done, and the moon is gone, you do not have any more 'moonlit lake' than when you were walking. Moonlit lake is somehow equally (but not absolutely uniformly in every way, each part is unique) embedded through a process, a practice. The practice does not assemble a finished product that then exists. 

Reading a book to a child (or otherwise telling them a story) might be another example. While you are storytelling, you are storytelling. At the beginning, you are storytelling just as much as right before you stop. When the song is over, there is no storytelling happening at all. On another level, you may develop new skills and talents over time as you tell more stories. But the first story you told is no less storytelling than the last. The goal is participation in storytelling, not an abstracted substance that is manufactured by storytelling. 

Bringing this analogy back to breathing, and being aware of your breathing: in some ways there is an equal value and contribution in your first zazen breaths as there are through and after a lifetime of dedicated practice. Some aspects are different, but some aspects are not. The moonlit lake is somehow equivalent across a process made of very different and unique moments and parts. 



Interpretation, Nuance & Presumption
A running theme in mindful-breathing-zazen practice is trying not to make too many presumptions, and learning to be aware of the presumptions that you did not know you had. 

This does not mean you should never make decisions or recognitions or judgement calls: if you realized you are meditating in a burning building, or on the coast just before a tsunami, you should immediately flee to safety. 

In each culture and time period there are probably concepts that pre-occupy various people. Maybe it is shame or pride or superiority or inferiority or guilt or resentment or some other agenda that appears so 'fish in water' normal that we do not even see that it is a preoccupation particular with the geitzgeist (~spirit of the times) in which we are swimming. 

In the USA such a background assumption is probably a return-on-investment ROI calculation: this zazen better be worthy my time, I better be getting something out of this zazen, I need to finish and achieve this zazen, this zazen needs to be a good accomplishment with a nice gold trophy and a certificate, I need to be getting better at this, I need to be winning something, I need to be getting that achievement, I need to move towards winning the zazen, I need to dominate all those other beginner noobs and have more zazen and better zazen than they do, etc. Or conversely: I can't do this. I'm just starting out. I don't feel anything happening. This isn't working. This will take so long to accomplish and I don't even know what that finished in-hand accomplishment of zazen looks like. This won't ever work, I'm just starting out. What should all this bio-feedback be finally producing? 

Another presumption may be that 'just breathing' or 'just being aware' does not involve fingers, and somehow requires isolation from the world. These two presumptions may both be calamitously backwards. 

As in the analogy of the moon and the lake for Dogen's concept of the start of a process being in some ways comparable to any other part of a process, many parts of zazen, or mindfulness, or signal-feedback, are in a process across which the starting steps are (if confusingly) as significant and any later steps.





#### Interpreting Old Books

There is a lot of valuable lore and research in old texts, but figuring out how you should interpret and utilize the information can take an unexpectedly convoluted and lengthy process. 

Here are three somewhat sequential works of ~literature / history that can form a path to where we are today.

1. Saigyo's writings (1118–1190) at the beginning of the Genpai period, expressing the value of nature and life and a need to integrate nature with society in wisdom. Saigyo is important for understanding the context of the times. He walked away from a seemingly  comfortable life as a noble (to everyone's confused surprise) ...just before the whole society erupted into decades of civil war. But he also wasn't content with status-quo abstract Buddhism, insisting on emphasizing a concrete connection to society and 'worldly' life.  

2. The Heike Monotagari (roughly ~1170-1200) about the Genpai period: one of the greatest expositions in all of literature about the value and substance of empty: a tale that is not only critical of the sport-maniac thrill-seeking that destroyed Japan, but also is highly critical (in my reading at least) of Buddhist practices that are overly disconnected from society, culture, nature, and positive value. The work has enormous value overall, and gives important context for the decisions made by Saigyo and Dogen.

From around 1300 into the 1970's, this set of stories was continually told by traveling musicians throughout Japan (perhaps similar to how the Iliad and the Odyssey are continually told in the west). This tale of social empathy, critical of deluded corrupt people and of abstract unhelpful buddhism, is a massive force in Japanese history. 

3. Just after the Genpei period: (1200-1253) Dogen wrote about and taught practices that aimed to help to prevent the disasters of the Genpei period. When Dogen writes about deluded judgement, clouded minds, "stupidy", and conversely about practices to improve consciousness and the world, this is not hallmark-card self-help rhetoric floating in space: there is a very, very real context for what he is saying. This is the equivalent of Jurgen Habermas (1929-2026) growing up in Nazi Germany to become a world force against the repeat or forgetting of fascist ruin.

The later few centuries seem to have built on this somewhat uniquely Japanese and uniquely pragmatic idea to design practice to integrate with the world (not separate from it) for positive outcomes (against pathology) producing ~literature that reinforces a society integrated into and connected to the world for positive culture and STEM: 
- Zeami (~1300), (creator of Noh, with his father, kanami)
- Sen-No Rikyu (to 1590),  (creator of the Japanese Tea Ceremony)

People today trying to look through historical koan commentary are in a similar position that people centuries from now will be in when looking through troves of public internet posts from 2010-2020: some useful information and a vast majority of anti-social trolling, hazing, and unprovoked hostility and cruelty. And, for some reason, many people seem to insist that everything in the record be treated as profound wisdom, even when it is literally two people threatening to beat each up in a show of escalating manlyness. Recognizing & Correcting Mistakes: Learning
As another theme-connecting example, the more than second half of the widely known (but not widely read) 'Leviathan' by Thomas Hobbes is an exploration attempting to understand learning, mind-consciousness, and signals within individuals and society: How do learning-machines work? How does information affect society? Needless to say, centuries later learning and mind are still a mystery, so Hobbes' exploration is largely a collection of questions and references in a collage of study, but it is not a great leap of assumption to presume that (unless he wrote and published the second half of the book by accident) he strongly felt that learning and mind were very important for societal functioning and preventing social collapse.

This is yet another example of mind-study research that followed a period of civil-war, upheaval, and societal collapse, inspiring survivors to try to understand what mistakes lead to bad-thinking, bad-perceptions, wrong-beliefs, misguided agendas, and societal collapse driven by delusions disconnected from reality. 


Koba Daishi did not live during a period of civil unrest when establishing Shingon Buddhism in Japan around the year 800 (early Heian), but it was during a time when Japan was trying a new governmental experiment, what Francis Fukuyama would probably call Japan's first attempt at a 'modern' nation-state republic administration that (for about 400 years) balanced regional and institutional influences. 

https://www.amazon.com/Origins-Political-Order-Prehuman-Revolution/dp/B006QGIQ94/ 
https://www.amazon.com/Political-Order-Decay-Industrial-Globalization/dp/0374535620/

A critical balance that, as Fukuyama outlines (or theorizes), all instances and types of societies need to maintain on various levels is avoiding state-distraction or state-capture by any particular interest, institution, or local-family-tribe interest (where there is a particular friction point between regional leaders (often called nobles, regents, lords, war-lords, big-men, chiefs, aristocrats, etc, in English) and an abstract administration shared across regions and institutions and which should be impartial and not biased by pathology. For centuries Shigon worked in coordination with both overall administration and with regional/local leaders as a pre-STEM academic wing of administration dynamics. This may be merely a historical curiosity, but it is interesting that Shingon existed as a kind of precursor to administrative statistics (state-data) in Japan, given the reputation of Buddhism as being anti-world or separate from the world.


#### Connection to the World
Despite the reputation the meditation and buddhism have as being remote, withdrawn, self-isolating, forcing the world and yourself out of your mind, etc., there are many examples and parallels here about the context of 21st century mindfulness being focused on an overt effort (if not a struggle and fight) to connect with and stay connected to the world, and having reliable data with which to operate and make decisions and evaluations (as well as other experiences). This is not a new, last-minute, pivot and re-interpretation of ancient ideas. At least in Japan this effort (to connect mind-focus to societal health) goes back almost a thousand years. And over and over we see key contributors having a life-context of working to understand and prevent societal disorientation and collapse because they lived through (or just after) a catastrophic societal collapse.
- Saigyo
- Dogen
- Hobbes
- Thich Nhat Hanh 


Parallel/Convergent Evolution, Non-Monophyletics, & Synchretism 
The current synthesis (speaking broadly of international interest in applying mindful-ness & meditation to health) of mindful / meditation things in the 2020's does not come from one single place, lineage, tradition, or discipline. 

I mention this because as you try to navigate the learning space and learning-curves of meditation, you will undoubtedly wonder: What are the sources? Where should I look for basic information about meditation and mindfulness? Where did it come from? Is there... a basic guide of steps? Who defined basic meditation practice historically? 

The main take-away for this section is: Be prepared to look in a few different (seemingly unrelated) places. I will try to explain and justify how this is so. 

While I think the historical narrative of humanity working towards healthy-mind and healthy-learning is somewhat progressive and coherent, it is more like 'international science' where people from many backgrounds and regions (and cultural traditions) come together on a general shared set of problems to solve: mind, learning, society, health (etc)

There are not a lot of terms in English for things that do not have one root, three are (and most of them are pejorative (negative) and suggest a lack of real-substance). 
- Parallel/Convergent Evolution
- Non-Monophyletics
- Synchretism

Parallel or convergent evolution is where the same 'thing' is arrived at from different paths, such as 'wings' 'eyes' 'leafs' or even 'body-plans' such as the cactus (well suited to dry areas). 

Non-Monophyletics (literally meaning: not from one tree) are groups such as Lilies, which (as in parallel-evolution) look the same to us but are made of un-related families that 'converged' on a form.

"Synchretism" is an often pejorative term used to refer to cultural traditions that emerge from a blending of other different traditions. Purists love to repeat their belief that anything syncretic or non-monophyletic is not real (avoiding topics such as 'eyes' and 'leafs,' lest they have to claim that eyes and leafs do not exist in the world in any way...)

World-Supporting mind-awareness health feedback is a convergent set of fields from many disciplines and groups, much like STEM:  the most 'synchretic' of all human domains is STEM itself... which is hardly lacking in rigorous substance (Syncretic STEM is on the other end of that spectrum, being too concerned with rigor to be broadly useful to everything in life).

#### "Is it connected?" not "Where did it come from?"
I mention this here, hopefully not just as a shameless pitch for my own research, but as an example of how information that came from somewhere completely unrelated to Zen-breathing can end up being part of the overall picture of how to use zazen-breathing and to contextualize for what: preventing system collapse appears to be, from many directions, one of the underlying themes (one might even say, imperatives and mandates). 


"The Orange [Fruit] Meditation"

To come full circle, Thich Nhat Hanh was a Vietnamese Buddhist monk (who passed away in 2022 at the age of 95) who is broadly credited and acknowledged as the founded and synthesizer of what in the 2020's was called the 'Mindfullness' movement, which has extended into secular scientific and fitness mindfulness (not needing to have any 'religious' element). 

How, when, where, and why did Thich Nhat Hanh create the concept of 'Mindfullness' and for what? As has been recounted many times (a standard in courses on mindfulness) (and so perhaps simplified for historical narrative), Thich Nhat Hanh was in Vietnam in the 1970's during the Vietnam war, and worked to help refugees feeling the violence after the defeat and collapse of South Vietnam at the end of the horrible war as they escaped in boats. He noticed that the mental state of people played a large factor in whether they survived (in whether groups of people survived), and that seeds of clarity and coherence from just one person on a boat could prevent the whole boat from collapsing into panic, despair, and ultimately perishing. This observation made a huge impact on him, and evolved into 'Mindfulness.'

So again: A clear and coherent connection between mind and reality, so as to prevent harmful collapse (driven by confused, disoriented, and reality-divorced mental states) is a very consistent and concrete theme in this narrative. (Not all of hinduism, or buddhism, or consciousness-research, have this focus, each of those have many many branches and sides.)

(Note: His broader humanity-supporting movement he called "Plum Village")

As a side note: Mindfulness emerged not from either a 'Zen' or 'esoteric-vajrayana' school, but from a branch usually more focused on highly simplified practices for the broader (often illiterate, historically) population (apologies for vast oversimplification) [usually translated into English as 'Pure Land Buddhism', which varies between Vietnamese and Japanese versions).

One of the most famous of Thich Nhat Hanh's 'mindfulness exercises' is the "Tangerine Meditation" (or '[eating an] Orange (citrus fruit) meditation'), where you spend an hour slowly and appreciatively and cognizantly and empathetically and clearly eating an orange, experiencing every aspect of the process involving the fruit (and where it come from) and your experience of eating it, and of your body during the process (and, note: not as a snarky nihilistic: 'you horrible monster, you are consuming a living fruit, what did it ever do to you!' way, or a defeatist "this is evil but, what can we do? [insinuating defeat]" way, but rather in an (almost ecstatically) positive, respectful, and art-appreciation way, where the whole process is healthy, sustainable, meaningful, valuable, functional, and beautiful, artistic, world-connected, culinary, poetic, literary, historical, social, etc. 

Some mindfulness can be very dry and clinical, but it is interesting to note that Thich Nhat Hanh was overflowing with enthusiastic passion and worldly interdisciplinary connections.

# Try three variables both ways:

1. Breath alone, and not alone.
Sometimes (maybe usually) breathe on your own. Occasionally, share breathing with a child, friend, or family member.

2. Breath motionless (sitting), and breath with a bead-set (sitting).
While very simple, the tactile tracing of beads while you breath can serve two very practical purposes:

A. It is ironically difficult to tell if you have done your daily ~100 zazen breaths... if you are shunning any contact with the world that would tell you how many times you watched a breath: you can't count. 

B. In innumerable nuanced ways, feeling one bead for each breath can help you to be aware of your breath, body and mind (which... is the goal). Just as the painter or musician uses their fingers in their expression and apprehension of the world, fingers (however strangely) are part of mind (and the cult of the disembodied brain is rarely helpful). As a navigation device like a compass to a hiker, a bead set can make a confusing landscape navigable.
